Xbox Live Guide for Producers

Summary

Xbox Live represents a massive, ongoing investment in the online gaming medium. For the first time in the history of online gaming, we created the back-end technology, capable of supporting a wide array of online games, before the creation of the content it would serve. This technology was developed in collaboration with publishers, developers, customers, business strategists, and marketing professionals. What this means for developers is that they can focus more on content and leave the onerous chores of networking, security, billing, and policy enforcement to the Xbox Live service.

The demand of console simplicity allows online game developers to think elegantly again, focusing on the core allure of the medium and, thus, breaking the expensive and risky cycle of ever more complex forms of one particular genre of online gaming: the cumulative character graphical MUD associated with massively multiplayer gaming today.

Further, Xbox Live is a broadband-only service. This not only offers better opportunities for our service to provide customers with good connections to each other, it also offers developers and publishers a viable means to deliver content to their customers.

Xbox Live Q&A

What makes the Xbox a viable online gaming platform?

More than merely viable, the Xbox® video game system from Microsoft is an ideal online game development platform. Developers of online games for personal computers have to support a wide range of home computer capabilities, myriad makes and models of video and hardware, motherboards, game peripherals, and constantly updated drivers for all of the above as well as cope with inherently insecure software and data streams. An Xbox online game developer writes for a single high-performance hardware platform, with built-in software media security, a hard disk, an Ethernet port, and multiple layers of network security on the Xbox Live service.

Why develop an online service for console games?

Unlike the vast majority of computer games, console gaming began as a social experience and remains so. Xbox Live offers console gamers a virtual couch, enabling them to play with their friends no matter where they live. Employing voice along with connectivity makes the distance between friends seem very short indeed. Plus, unlike most PC games, Xbox Live gives players complete control over whom they choose to game with. In short, it's a logical extension of the core of console gaming.

Sounds good in theory, but do console gamers really want this?

We interviewed thousands of console game players. Not only did the vast majority want the ability to play console games online, they were very specific about the features they wanted an online console game service to offer. Their strongest desires became our core feature set for the service.

It's good to want, but how many Xbox owners have broadband, much less the ability to connect their Xbox consoles to it?

Currently, 40 percent of Xbox owners have broadband connections, and—much to our surprise—the majority of these have their broadband connection in the same room where they keep their Xbox. As for physically connecting to our service, the Xbox has always had an Ethernet port and, thus, no need for added peripherals to connect an Xbox to the Internet.

Certainly there's more to it than that.

From the first we realized that Xbox Live had to be simple to use in keeping with console gaming. The only cumbersome portion of the Xbox Live experience is the initial account setup, and we have reduced that process to five minutes or fewer. Once they have established an account, all they need to do is pop in a Xbox Live-enabled Xbox game DVD and select Xbox Live, and they are online with your game of choice.

Sounds good, but how do I make money? You get the entire subscription fee.

Initially, that's quite true because our research and projections indicate that this is the break-even price point for us to establish and operate the service. The money to be made by publishers and developers comes from four avenues:

  1. The differentiation and perceived added value of the Xbox Live label on Xbox titles. When customers are evaluating games to buy, the Xbox Live label will appeal not only to current or eventual Xbox Live customers but also to customers who may not be or never will become subscribers yet want that functionality in case they do elect to subscribe.


  2. Longer shelf life. Online games are played longer than stand-alone games, plus online titles create a lingering buzz in online gamer communities that few stand-alone titles can. This serves to create customers who may have passed on a title when it first hit the shelves. This is why online titles tend to have a longer shelf life than stand-alone games.


  3. Downloadable content. Publishers can offer additional game content—new levels, new game items, expansion packs—to Xbox Live subscribers. This can be free or downloaded by customers for an added fee.


  4. Premium content. Inevitably, online console titles will evolve into games containing richer content with ever more persistent elements aimed at a longer player life cycle than a typical online game. Such games can command higher subscription fees, a hefty portion of which goes to the publisher.

You speak of security, yet someone has already hacked an Xbox and you plan to put Xbox consoles online.

A computer scientist devising an elaborate scheme to make an Xbox run other applications is a far cry from hackers penetrating Xbox Live. Security is not only crucial to customers, who will abandon any online game in which cheating and hacking are employed, it's essential to our partners and our business. Being able to trust the client saves enormous development and bandwidth resources. Every Xbox has a unique ID, security on the hardware itself, and communication that is signed, authenticated, validated, and encrypted. Any Xbox that exhibits abnormal communications behavior can be shut off instantly from our data centers. Our service accepts communication only from Xbox consoles. While we'd be foolish to assert that no one will be able to spoof an Xbox, it would take an effort beyond most hackers, and our hardware security schemes will constantly vary through each production run of Xbox consoles. In addition, we can auto-update specific games that have security holes and alter our encryption schemes.

Xbox Live in Greater Detail

What Customers Want

Customers want the best gaming experience. Our fundamental feature set for launch represents the fondest wishes of thousands of console game players we interviewed:

  • A single identity for all games on the service. Contrary to conventional wisdom, most online game players want pseudonymity, not anonymity. Having a persistent game persona adds value to players and identification with the service. The more data you can associate with each player's persona, the more value you create for the player. Our service allows players to select a service-wide name. Our statistics servers allow you, the developer, to associate game related data with players.


  • The ability to find and make friends. As noted earlier, the virtual couch is perhaps the most important value proposition for console gamers seeking to go online. The presence and notification system offered by Xbox Live allows players to easily find their friends and invite them to game sessions. It also allows players to harvest positive connections they've made with players previously unknown to them. After each session, players can see a list of who was in the session with them. They can invite players to join their list of friends. Or, in the case of encountering players they decidedly did not like, they can choose to mute or block a player from attending future game sessions they create.


  • Voice. Although voice has been a feature of online games since the mid-1990s, its acceptance by players has been limited due to the quality of network speech and its overhead in a competitive game environment. Employing the combined benefits of broadband, single-source audio hardware, and highly refined and optimized codecs for voice along with voice fonts that allow players to alter and customize how their voices sound, voice in Xbox Live is an experience apart from any known on the PC. It gives immediacy and humanity to gaming that simply cannot be achieved with a keyboard. It also provides an avenue for player harassment, which is why any player can mute any other player whose speech he or she finds offensive.


  • Matchmaking. Players must be able to find and join a game session that suits them with ease and speed. If they're looking for quick action, they can select QuickMatch. This returns a list of available game sessions based on limited search parameters. If they're looking for something more specific, they can select OptiMatch. This will return a list of available sessions based on a search containing a larger set of parameters that the players select. If they want to host their own session, players can arrange a session and advertise it. If they want to host a private session for just their friends, they can arrange it and invite their friends who are online to join in.


  • Ease in finding high-quality connections. Xbox Live employs proximity probing software to determine the viability of game sessions for players. Quality of connection is returned with matchmaking queries in an iconic form that's far easier to understand than ping times and variance statistics.

What Developers Want

Developers want a high-performance, reliable platform; robust, optimized online development software; and an unwavering commitment from Microsoft.

We provide Xbox Live content developers with more than single-source hardware that console studios have always relied upon:

  • The Xbox Live XDK. In addition to providing the libraries you need to enable Xbox Live features, our XDK also comes with a written guide to creating games for the service, as well as sample code for all the Xbox Live features along with interface examples.


  • Developer support. Our Advanced Technology Group (ATG) not only knows the Xbox Live libraries and the Xbox thoroughly, most of them come from the game development community. In addition to providing technical support and guidance, they can also review your game for performance and suggest optimizations if they see opportunities for improvement.


  • Connection to Partner Net. At any time during the development of your game, you can test your code against Partner Net—a scaled down but fully functional example of our data centers.

What Microsoft Wants

Microsoft wants to create a console online game service that is more advanced, more powerful, and more sophisticated than any online entertainment endeavor ever undertaken.

  • Microsoft is not only investing heavily to launch Xbox Live, the company is also committed for the long term.


  • We are building four data centers: London, Seattle, Tokyo, and Tukwila.


  • A subset of these—our local center hosting Partner Net, Cert Net, and Test Net—contains:

    • 348 computers

    • 677 processors

    • 474 gigabytes of RAM

    • 24.348 terabytes of hard disk space

    • 43.2 miles of cable

    • 18,433 pounds of equipment, not including tables, chairs, and monitors


  • Microsoft has plans for ongoing development and expansion of the service, post launch, aimed at growing and expanding the medium into richer feature sets, new genres, a broader customer base, and innovative forms of interactive entertainment not available today.

The Opportunity for Game Developers

For you, as console game producers, we have made an enormous, informed investment aimed at:

  • Lowering the previously enormous barrier to adding online features to your games.


  • Enabling you to plan and schedule online functionality reliably.


  • Allowing you to innovate in the online medium and set your title apart from the competition in new ways.


  • Gaining valuable feedback and data directly from your customers, thus better informing your subsequent game development efforts.


  • Retaining excitement for your games beyond the normal store shelf life cycle.
